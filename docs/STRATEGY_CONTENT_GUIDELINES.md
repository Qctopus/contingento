# Strategy Content Guidelines
## Writing Effective SME-Focused Strategy Content for Caribbean Businesses

---

## üéØ Philosophy

This system is **NOT** a generic disaster planning tool. It's a **Caribbean SME-focused business continuity advisor** that:
- Speaks directly to small business owners in plain language
- Provides realistic, affordable options for resource-limited businesses
- Includes real examples from Caribbean businesses
- Respects cultural and economic context

---

## ‚úçÔ∏è Writing SME Titles

### ‚úÖ DO:
- **Use benefit-driven language**: "Protect Your Business from Hurricane Damage"
- **Address the business owner directly**: "Your", "You Can"
- **Focus on outcomes**: What they achieve, not what they do
- **Keep it conversational**: Like talking to a friend

### ‚ùå DON'T:
- **Use technical jargon**: "Hurricane Preparedness & Property Protection"
- **Be formal or corporate**: "Implementation of Storm Mitigation Strategies"
- **Use passive voice**: "Strategies for Hurricane Protection"
- **Make it about the process**: "How to Prepare for Hurricanes"

### Examples:
```
‚ùå Data Backup and Recovery Systems
‚úÖ Protect Your Business Records from Disasters

‚ùå Emergency Financial Reserve Management
‚úÖ Build a Cash Reserve for When Disaster Strikes

‚ùå Supply Chain Risk Mitigation Procedures
‚úÖ Never Run Out of Stock Because One Supplier Failed
```

## üìù Writing SME Summaries

**Goal**: One or two sentences that explain WHY this matters in plain language.

### Formula:
1. **State the problem** (what Caribbean SMEs face)
2. **Show the solution** (what this strategy does)
3. **Promise the outcome** (what they'll achieve)

### ‚úÖ Good Examples:
```
"Power outages are common in the Caribbean - from storms to grid failures. 
Having backup power means you can keep serving customers, protect perishables, 
and maintain security when others go dark."

"Flash floods can ruin inventory, damage equipment, and close your business 
for weeks. Simple flood prevention saves thousands in losses and gets you 
back in business faster."
```

### ‚ùå Bad Examples:
```
"This strategy addresses the implementation of comprehensive backup power 
systems for business continuity."
‚Üí Problem: Corporate speak, no Caribbean context, doesn't say WHY

"Flooding is a risk that should be mitigated through proper preparation."
‚Üí Problem: Passive, vague, no specific benefits
```

## ‚úÖ Writing Benefit Bullets

**Goal**: 3-5 concrete outcomes that a business owner cares about.

### Rules:
1. Start with **action verbs** or outcomes
2. Focus on **business results** (money, time, customers)
3. Be **specific** - avoid vague terms
4. Make it **personal** - "your staff", "your inventory"

### ‚úÖ Good Examples:
```
‚Ä¢ Reopen faster than competitors after the storm
‚Ä¢ Protect expensive equipment and stock
‚Ä¢ Stay open and making money during power outages
‚Ä¢ Keep customers confident with visible safety measures
```

### ‚ùå Bad Examples:
```
‚Ä¢ Improved operational resilience
‚Üí Problem: Vague, corporate speak

‚Ä¢ Reduced vulnerability to supply chain disruptions
‚Üí Problem: Technical, doesn't say what owner gets

‚Ä¢ Enhanced preparedness levels
‚Üí Problem: Meaningless - "enhanced" how?
```

## üíö Writing Real Caribbean Success Stories

**Goal**: Show a real Caribbean business that benefited from this strategy with specific numbers and places.

### Required Elements:
1. **Location** - Specific Jamaican town/parish
2. **Business type** - What kind of business
3. **Year/event** - When this happened (recent is better)
4. **Specific costs** - JMD amounts
5. **Clear outcome** - What happened vs what would have happened

### Template:
```
[Event] happened in [Location] in [Year]. [Business type] that had 
[this strategy] [positive outcome with JMD amount]. [Business without 
strategy] [negative outcome with JMD amount]. [Cost of strategy] in 
[investment] saved/made [specific amount].
```

### ‚úÖ Good Example:
```
"A pharmacy in May Pen installed a JMD 180,000 generator in 2023. 
During a 3-day outage in July, they were the only pharmacy open in the area. 
They made JMD 450,000 in extra sales while competitors lost inventory to 
spoilage. The generator paid for itself in one weekend."
```

**Why it works**:
- Specific place (May Pen)
- Specific business (pharmacy)
- Specific time (2023, July)
- Specific costs (JMD 180,000 investment, JMD 450,000 return)
- Clear comparison (they stayed open, others closed)
- Relatable outcome (paid for itself)

### ‚ùå Bad Example:
```
"Many businesses have found that backup power systems provide significant 
value during outages."
```

**Why it fails**:
- No specific location
- No specific business
- No specific numbers
- No story
- No Caribbean context

## üí∞ Writing Cost Estimates (JMD)

### Rules:
1. **Always use JMD** - not USD, not "medium cost"
2. **Give ranges** - "JMD 15,000-80,000" (accounts for variation)
3. **Be realistic** - based on actual Caribbean prices
4. **Include context** - what that money covers

### ‚úÖ Good Examples:
```
"JMD 15,000-80,000 (shutters, supplies, securing)"
"JMD 50,000-250,000 (depending on solution)"
"JMD 5,000-50,000 (basic to comprehensive)"
```

### Cost Ranges by Business Size:
- **Micro** (1-5 employees): JMD 5,000-50,000
- **Small** (6-20 employees): JMD 50,000-200,000
- **Medium** (21-50 employees): JMD 200,000-1M+

## üí∏ Writing Low Budget Alternatives

**Goal**: Show the cheapest way to implement this, specific to Caribbean SMEs.

### Rules:
1. **Be specific** - exact items and costs
2. **DIY-friendly** - something they can actually do
3. **Still effective** - not just "do nothing"
4. **Caribbean-available** - can buy locally

### ‚úÖ Good Example:
```
"Start with a small inverter (JMD 15,000) and car batteries (JMD 10,000 each) 
to power essentials like cash register, internet router, and a few lights. 
Total: JMD 35,000-50,000."
```

**Why it works**:
- Specific items (inverter, car batteries)
- Specific costs for each
- Says what it can power
- Total cost calculated
- Realistic for micro businesses

### ‚ùå Bad Example:
```
"Consider lower-cost alternatives that may provide adequate protection."
```

**Why it fails**:
- No specifics
- Vague ("consider", "may")
- No costs
- No guidance

---

## üîß Writing DIY Approaches

**Goal**: Step-by-step instructions a business owner can follow themselves.

### Format:
Use numbered steps with specific actions and costs.

### ‚úÖ Good Example:
```
"1) Buy plywood sheets and hinges (JMD 8,000)
2) Cut to fit your windows
3) Paint with exterior paint (JMD 2,000)
4) Install simple hinges so they fold down when not needed
Total DIY cost: ~JMD 12,000 vs JMD 50,000+ for professional metal shutters"
```

**Why it works**:
- Numbered steps
- Specific materials and costs
- Clear instructions
- Compares to professional option
- Shows savings

### ‚ùå Bad Example:
```
"You can implement this strategy yourself by following standard procedures 
and best practices."
```

**Why it fails**:
- No steps
- No specifics
- No costs
- No actionable guidance

4. **Caribbean context** - relevant locally

### ‚ùå Bad Examples:

‚Ä¢ Ensure proper maintenance

## üå¥ Caribbean Context

### Cultural Considerations:
- **Direct communication** - Caribbeans appreciate straightforward advice
- **Community-focused** - Mention word-of-mouth, asking neighbors
- **Practical over theoretical** - Show them how, not just why
- **Cost-conscious** - Always provide budget options
- **Relationship-based** - Emphasize building supplier relationships

### Economic Reality:
- **Cash-constrained** - Most SMEs have tight budgets
- **Informal sector** - Many operate informally
- **Limited staff** - Owner does everything in micro businesses
- **Seasonal fluctuations** - Tourism, agriculture vary by season
- **Supply challenges** - Getting materials can be difficult

### Common Tools/Resources:
- **WhatsApp** - Primary business communication
- **Cash/check** - Many still don't use digital payments
- **Local suppliers** - Prefer local over imported when possible
- **DIY culture** - Strong tradition of doing things yourself
- **Family involvement** - Family often helps in business

### Caribbean-Specific Hazards:
- **Hurricane season** - June to November
- **Flooding** - Especially during tropical storms
- **Power outages** - Common, often prolonged
- **Water shortages** - Periodic, especially during drought
- **Supply disruptions** - Shipping delays, import challenges

## üö´ What NOT to Do

### DON'T:
1. **Use corporate speak** - "implement comprehensive mitigation strategies"
2. **Be vague** - "take appropriate measures"
3. **Ignore costs** - "invest in proper equipment"
4. **Assume resources** - "hire a consultant"
5. **Use generic examples** - "many businesses find this useful"
6. **Forget Caribbean context** - examples from US/Europe
7. **Be overly technical** - "deploy redundant infrastructure"
8. **Use passive voice** - "procedures should be established"

---

## ‚úÖ Content Review Process

Before finalizing content:

1. **Read it aloud** - Does it sound natural?
2. **Show it to a business owner** - Do they understand it?
3. **Check costs** - Are JMD amounts realistic?
4. **Verify locations** - Are places real and correct?
5. **Test instructions** - Can someone follow the DIY steps?
6. **Check language** - Is it conversational and clear?
7. **Verify Caribbean context** - Is it locally relevant?

---

## üìû Questions?

When in doubt:
- **Simpler is better** - Clarity over sophistication
- **Specific is better** - "JMD 15,000" not "low cost"
- **Local is better** - "May Pen" not "a town"
- **Conversational is better** - "You can" not "One should"

**Remember**: You're writing for a busy Caribbean business owner who needs practical, affordable, actionable advice. Not for academics or consultants.
